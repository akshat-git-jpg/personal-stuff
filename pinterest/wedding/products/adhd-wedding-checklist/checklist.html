A kinder way to plan

If a normal wedding checklist makes you freeze, you're not lazy and you're not behind — it's just too much at once. This one is built differently: tiny steps, no clutter, and full permission to do it your way. A few rules that make planning ADHD-friendly:

1. One tiny thing at a time

Ignore the whole list. Find the next unchecked box and just do that. That's the entire method.

2. Get it out of your head

Brain-dump everything (next page) so it stops rattling around. On paper, it's smaller.

3. Limit your choices

Infinite options = paralysis. Pick from 2–3, then stop looking. Done beats perfect.

4. Let your phone remember

Set a reminder/alarm the second you think of a task. Don't trust your memory — trust the alarm.

5. Body-double & delegate

Do boring tasks next to a friend (in person or on video), and hand off whatever you can.

6. Reward every win

Checked a box? That counts. Celebrate small — momentum is everything.

The only rule

You do not have to do this in order, all at once, or perfectly. Find one box. Do it. Come back tomorrow. That's planning a wedding with ADHD — and it works.

What actually helps

Systems for an ADHD brain

  • The 2-minute rule. If a task takes under 2 minutes, do it the moment you think of it. Don't write it down — just do it.
  • One calendar, with alarms. Every deposit, fitting, and deadline goes in your phone with an alert. Your brain is for ideas, not storage.
  • Body-doubling. Boring admin (RSVPs, emails) feels impossible alone — do it on a video call with a friend, or at a café.
  • Delegate one whole thing. Hand an entire category (favors, playlist, hotel block) to someone who offered to help. Off your plate completely.
  • Limit the rabbit holes. Give yourself 3 options and a deadline. When the timer goes off, you decide. No more tabs.
  • Make it visible. Print this, stick it on the fridge. Out of sight really is out of mind — so keep it in sight.
  • Be kind to yourself. You will forget things. That's what the lists and your point person are for. It will still be a beautiful day.
