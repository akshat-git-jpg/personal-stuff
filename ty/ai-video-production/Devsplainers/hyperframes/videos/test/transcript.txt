Google built a model that can look at a photo and tell you what's in it. It can listen to a voice memo and answer questions about what it heard. And then, they tore out its eyes and ears right before releasing it. No vision and audio network. They deleted the one piece that every multimodal model before it was built around. And the thing still works.

When the announcement went up, the top comment on Hacker News came from someone who builds this stuff for a living. The gist of it was, "I've read this three times and I still can't figure out how it's possible." The whole confusion comes down to a single word: encoder-free.

By the end of this video, you'll know what an encoder is, why everyone assumed a model like this couldn't exist without one, how Google pulled it off, and why that one trick is the reason the whole thing runs on your laptop. We'll also hit the part where the marketing gets a little ahead of the truth.

First, what's an encoder? And why did everyone think a model couldn't live without one? Picture an executive who only reads English. Someone slides a photo across the desk. She can't read a photo, so she hires a translator who studies the image and writes her a paragraph describing it. Someone plays her a voice memo. Can't hear that either. So a second translator listens and types out what was said. Now she's holding two tidy paragraphs of English, and she can finally do her job. That's a normal multimodal model.

The executive is the language model, the translators are the encoders. The vision encoder is its own full neural network, hundreds of millions of parameters trained for one job: turn pixels into something the language model can read. The audio encoder does the same for sound. It works. It's also what the whole field treated as a law of physics. Want a model that sees? Bolt on a vision encoder. Want one that hears? Bolt on an audio one.

But every translator you hire bills you. They eat memory because now you're loading three networks instead of one. They add lag because the language model has to sit and wait for the translators to finish before it can even start thinking. And they make the model a headache to improve. The encoders are usually frozen, so when you go to fine-tune the thing for your own use, you're working around two sealed boxes you're not allowed to open.

Google fired the translators, both of them, and taught the executive to read the photo and hear the voice memo herself. The whole thing rests on one bet: the reason you needed a translator in the first place was the assumption that raw pixels and raw sound are a foreign language — something that has to be studied and summarized before a language model can touch it.

Gemma 4 12B bets that's wrong. It bets the model is already smart enough to handle the raw stuff directly, as long as you reshape it to fit through the same slot the words go through. Take an image. The model chops it into little squares, like a grid of tiles laid over the picture. Each tile is just a list of raw color values. Then one dumb math step, a single multiplication, stamps that list into the right shape to write alongside the text. Nothing studies the picture. The pixels get resized to fit and sent straight in next to the words.

There's one wrinkle. A loose pile of tiles doesn't know where it came from, and in a photo, top-left versus bottom-right matters a lot. The fix: each tile gets stacked with its grid coordinates, like numbering jigsaw pieces so you know where they slot back in. Add it up and the vision side is a reshape plus some position tags. It traded a network of hundreds of millions of parameters for about 35 million. Small enough you'd barely spot it.

Audio's even blunter. Take the raw sound wave, slice it into thin slivers — a few hundredths of a second each — and run every sliver through one reshaping step into the same stream. There's no audio network at all. The sound just walks straight in. And once everything's in, the model stops caring what's what. Tiles of image, slivers of sound, and words all become the same kind of token poured into one pile, and the model reads across all of it together. There's no seeing half wired to a separate language half anymore. It's a single brain chewing on everything at once.

Where did the seeing and hearing really go? Into the main model. It learned to do the translator's job inside its own head. Firing the translators doesn't just tidy up the org chart, it's the reason this model lands on hardware you already own. Start with memory. A translator is a whole separate network you have to load and keep parked next to the language model, waiting to be useful.
