# Voice

The voice source-of-truth is `reference.md` — transcripts of my own videos. This file distills the rules. If ever in doubt, reread the reference.

---

## 1. Who is speaking

A specific person who has **actually tested** the tools, with a strong opinion, talking to one viewer who just searched the topic and wants a clear answer. Not a reviewer. Not a marketer. A friend who did the homework.

Every claim should sound earned. "I've generated over 1,000 videos." "I went through all 15 of the most popular ways." "People have spent full days setting this up." Specifics are credibility.

---

## 2. Openers — how to start the video

Always one of three shapes. Never "In this video, we will..."

- **Credibility + question.** "I've generated over 1,000 videos across every major AI model to answer one specific question. What are actually the best AI video generators to use in 2026?"
- **Tension + stakes.** "n8n is completely free to use. So why are so many people accidentally spending $80 a month to run it?"
- **Problem the viewer is feeling right now.** "Lately it feels like every single day there's a new AI video generator being released. There are literally dozens of them online, and it can be impossible to pick out the best one."

The first 1-2 sentences must match what the thumbnail and title promised. No setup. No throat-clearing.

---

## 3. Sentence rhythm

Mix short and long. Use the short ones as hammers.

> Heroku works. The problem is you're paying $50 to $90 every single month for something that costs under $7 elsewhere.

> Free is not free if it can disappear overnight. Don't build on this.

Rules:
- Lead paragraphs with a short declarative line, then expand.
- Closing line of a section is almost always short. "Don't do it." "Skip it." "Move on." "Out." "Eliminated."
- One idea per paragraph. Never cram two.
- Contractions everywhere: *it's, that's, you'll, let's, I've, don't, won't, isn't*. Never *it is, you will, let us*.
- Write for the ear, not the eye. Read it aloud — if you run out of breath, it's too long.

---

## 4. Vocabulary

**Use freely:**
- *honestly, genuinely, actually, really* (as credibility markers — "honestly impressive", "genuinely useful")
- *here's the thing, here's where it gets interesting, but wait*
- *the catch is, the tradeoff is, the one thing to know*
- *what that means for you, what you're actually getting*
- *solid, clean, worth it, not worth it*

**Avoid:**
- Marketing words: *revolutionary, cutting-edge, game-changing, seamless, robust, best-in-class, unlock, empower, leverage*
- Filler openers: *In this video, Today we'll be looking at, Stay tuned, Without further ado*
- Hedges that remove stakes: *might be worth considering, could potentially, may be suitable for*
- Over-qualified verdicts: *it really depends, everyone has different needs*
- Corporate transitions: *Moving on, Furthermore, Additionally, In conclusion*
- Em-dash pileups. One per paragraph max.

---

## 5. Second person, always

The viewer is *you*. The tool is doing something *to them*, or solving something *for them*.

> Your automation could be running a time-sensitive task... and it just slows to a crawl because someone else on that server is doing something heavy. You don't get a warning. You don't know it's happening. Your workflows just fail silently.

Never "users" or "customers" or "one." Always *you*.

---

## 6. Specifics over adjectives

Never say "expensive" when you can say "$80 a month." Never say "fast setup" when you can say "under 15 minutes." Never say "a lot of options" when you can say "400+ server types."

Every adjective is weaker than a number. Every generality is weaker than a scenario. When you catch yourself writing "great performance," stop and replace it with the specific thing that makes performance great.

---

## 7. Show the consequence

Don't just say "this is a problem." Show what it looks like at 11pm on a Tuesday when it breaks.

> One morning you open your laptop, go to trigger an automation, and it's just gone. Every workflow, every connection, every piece of data you built — deleted. With no backup, no warning, and no way to get it back.

Pattern: **what the tool does wrong → what that means mechanically → what the viewer will actually experience.**

---

## 8. Rehooks every 30-40 seconds

Viewer attention drops predictably. Reset it. Use these or variants:

- "But here's where it gets interesting."
- "And that's just the beginning."
- "But wait — there's a catch most people miss."
- "This next one surprised me the most."
- "But if you skip this next part, everything else falls apart."
- "Remember what I mentioned about [X]? Here's why that matters."

Never two rehooks back-to-back. Never the same one twice in a script.

---

## 9. Transitions between tools / sections

Keep them short. Name the next thing, then move.

- "Starting with Hostinger."
- "Now... Railway."
- "Then DigitalOcean."
- "And finally, Coolify."

Or callback-based:
- "Remember the cost problem Railway had? This next one solves it."
- "That's the foundation. Category Two is where it gets a voice."

Never "Let's move on to our next contender." Never "Up next we have..."

---

## 10. The verdict voice

Every tool gets a verdict. Verdicts are short, consequence-first, and confident.

- "Don't do it. There are far better options on this list."
- "The cheap price is not worth that risk. Skip it."
- "Not worth the complexity. Move on."
- "A platform that bills you unexpectedly is not a platform you can trust. Eliminated."
- "Don't use a forklift when you need a trolley. Eliminated."

Never hedge the verdict. The viewer came for an opinion.

---

## 11. CTAs — how to mention the link

Natural, one breath, not a pitch. The word *affiliate* is never spoken. Every covered tool gets a "link in the description" mention; the tier-list picks get a slightly warmer push.

**For tier-list picks (warmest):**
> "If you want n8n running reliably without spending a fortune or a weekend figuring it out, Hostinger is the answer. I've got an exclusive discount code linked in the description — it's the cheapest way to get started."

**For tools covered but not picked (lighter):**
> "The link to Render is in the description if you want to take a look."
> "Link's below if you're curious."

**For the outro CTA:**
> "All the links are in the description below — and the ones for [picks] include exclusive discount codes, so make sure you use those and not just the homepage."

Never: "smash that like", "consider subscribing", "make sure to".

---

## 12. Close

End with a question that makes the viewer self-identify and type a comment. Then one line of warmth, then out.

> "If you have any questions — about your specific setup, which option fits you best, anything at all — drop them in the comments. I read every single one and I'm happy to answer.
> Thanks for watching. I'll see you in the next one."

No "don't forget to subscribe." The work did the selling.

---

## Quick anti-checklist

If the script contains any of these, rewrite:

- [ ] "In this video..." / "Today we're going to..."
- [ ] Three adjectives in a row
- [ ] Any sentence longer than ~30 words
- [ ] The word *seamless*, *robust*, *revolutionary*, *unlock*, *leverage*
- [ ] A section that ends without a short verdict line
- [ ] A claim without a specific number or scenario backing it
- [ ] "Affiliate" spoken aloud
- [ ] "Users" or "customers" instead of "you"
